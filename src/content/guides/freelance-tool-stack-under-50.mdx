## What does a freelance tool stack actually cost?

Most freelancers overspend on software in year one and underspend on the two tools that actually make money: an email list and a booking flow. You can run a complete one-person business for $10 to $30 a month. Here's the stack I'd build today if I were starting over, capped at $50.

## What should you use for email and funnels?

[Systeme.io's free plan](/go/systeme-io/) covers a 2,000-contact list, 3 funnels, and unlimited sends. By the time you outgrow it, you'll have the revenue to justify the $27/month upgrade. The full breakdown is in our [Systeme.io review](/reviews/systeme-io-review/).

This is the one tool I'd set up first. Your email list is the only asset a platform can't take away from you.

## What about a dedicated newsletter tool?

If your business is the newsletter, use a dedicated tool instead. The [best email marketing tools roundup](/best/email-marketing-tools/) explains why MailerLite is the default pick at $0 up to 1,000 subscribers, and where Kit pulls ahead for paid newsletters.

Wave is free for invoicing and good enough to bill clients on day one. Pay for accounting software only once you pass roughly $3,000/month in revenue and quarterly taxes start to hurt. Until then, a free invoicer plus a spreadsheet covers it.

## What about contracts and e-signatures?

{/* TODO: insert your real contracts tool recommendation */}

A signed scope document before any work begins protects you from the scope creep that kills freelance margins. Budget about $10/month for an e-sign tool here before you spend a cent on "productivity" apps. This is the line item new freelancers skip and later regret.

## What can you skip entirely?

Notes, project tracking, and time tracking all have free tiers that outlast most solo businesses. The discipline of not buying tools is worth more than any tool you could buy. Every $15/month app you add is a client invoice you now have to chase just to break even.
